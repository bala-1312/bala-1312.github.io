On Childhood2025 · 09

This would grow up in time. So read at your leisure...

Rarely do we see the innocence of childhood as a gift, that feeling of the worst fear in your life was 'failing a subject', the stakes pretty high, back then. At least, in my life.
No, I'm not feeling nostalgic or melodramatic that it was gone.
But the realisation that how small that world was, only your school, neighbourhood, and the occasional trips. Yet how filled in that world was!
Banters, pranks, tournaments, and exams. You hate showing up on Monday, but only until you see your friend, then it would be all fun. I wonder that's how everybody felt! There were incidents that supports this, we would message others, letting them know in advance of our absence. This results in a particular ring of friends being absent.
Jealousy was rare. Factions made up for diversity, and the worst insult anyone could throw was foul language (until it wasn't).

I would wait eagerly for the evening Basketball practice, wondering at how could someone (coach) easily take a three pointer, I remember the time when he would be conversing with us and just a glance towards the board and 'whoosh' the sound of his shooting action so soft, it feels like a breeze on a sunny yet partly cloudy day.
And to the thing that was my addiction back then, the sound of the ball slipping clean through the net, untouched by rim or backboard. A perfect swish.
That was an audio-visual odyssey. I called it the "Ring shot".

That is when we learnt to be different in our trigger movement, lay-up or lay-in, jump shot.
These would become our special move, our identity. All of us trying different things at our drill session; the frequency jumped whenever girls were around (nothing conspicuous about it!).
Fridays would be a full 1-hour jog. Saturday is what everyone looks forward to; game day. You would beg for a substitution halfway through the game.

I have this memory of a match in my fifth or sixth grade, the referee was my P.T, I got a pass near the half-court and I dribbled down to our attacking post, I told him that I would convert this shot, it was a lay-up. (I always had a sweet spot for it; even though I rarely ever used it later in my life.)
There is something so essentially different from lay-up and lay-in.
It's a whole different feel when you go for a lay-up; one shows the ball in his palm to the post, almost like an offer of the kindest intention. You don't make any noise, neither does the board.
Yep, I made that shot!
